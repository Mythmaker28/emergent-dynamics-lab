# Three hostile peer reviews

These reviews intentionally adopt skeptical positions and are not endorsements.

## Reviewer 1 — “The ownership criterion is invented, underpowered, and overinterpreted”

The paper’s central word, “ownership,” is not a recognized measurable property. The authors manufacture a conjunction of five decoder scopes and then announce a philosophical conclusion when two noisy contrasts cross zero. That is not falsification; it is a failure to reject with only 21 selected worlds. L-over-E has a positive point estimate of 0.207 and L-over-B 0.145. Both intervals are wide. A larger study could easily make them positive. The title and conclusion turn an underpowered test into “persistence without ownership.”

The selection problem is serious. Only 21 of 50 worlds are analyzed. The manuscript calls the criteria frozen, but conditional inference on worlds that successfully track and turn over may generate a peculiar subset. We are not given enough information about why the other 29 fail or whether validity correlates with the strength of memory. Without an intention-to-analyze sensitivity, the result may characterize only easy-to-track droplets.

The permutation test shows that L contains information, but the access-scope comparisons are decoder-dependent. A ridge decoder on hand-chosen summaries cannot establish where information “belongs.” E and B may be easier or harder to decode for arbitrary representational reasons. The authors admit finite scopes, which undermines the central label. If distributed ownership is also false, what exactly owns the state? The answer seems to be that the experiment cannot tell.

The direct engineering of the feeding response makes the causal gate nearly tautological. Removing a field that explicitly modulates uptake changes uptake. Calling this “causal persistence” is technically true but not biologically interesting. The ablation checks do not rescue the conceptual circularity.

Finally, the historical narrative consumes too much space. Leakage, teleportation, fusion, and authorization defects suggest an unstable project, not exemplary transparency. I would reject unless the title is softened to “A preregistered decoder-scope test is inconclusive about local ownership” and the discussion explicitly treats low power and scope arbitrariness as the main result.

## Reviewer 2 — “The system cannot support claims about memory or individuality”

This is an engineered leaky field in a simulation. New material inherits local intensive memory by construction. The field is then wired into feeding. The paper asks whether this demonstrates memory, but the only reasonable answer was known before running anything: no. There is no active reconstruction, attractor, error correction, self-production, or adaptive use. The result is therefore a demonstration that passive copying plus a designed readout is passive copying plus a designed readout.

The manuscript repeatedly invokes biological identity, autopoiesis, droplets, protocells, and synthetic memory. Those references confer significance that the model has not earned. A 64×64 lattice with hand-built fields is not a protocell. “Deep turnover” refers to a diagnostic tracer in a computational rule, not measured molecular replacement. “Individual” refers to connected components under a tracker. “Own history” is an imposed label. The conceptual vocabulary is inflated even if caveats appear later.

The local causal confirmation is especially weak. The own intervention changes a directly coupled variable; sham and neighbour edits do not. That is exactly what local code should do. The prospective seal and elaborate authorization machinery are irrelevant to whether the scientific question is compelling. They risk turning software ceremony into epistemic authority.

The V4.1 result should not appear as supportive evidence. Three worlds are plainly insufficient, h2 is strongly negative, and earlier fusion handling cannot be reconstructed. The corrected positive h1 point estimate could be chance or leakage not yet found. Keeping it in the narrative invites readers to infer continuity where none is certified.

I would consider a methods paper that presents a hierarchy of tests and uses this system as a worked negative example. I would not accept the current framing as an empirical result about individual memory. The authors should remove “simulated droplets” from the biological lineage, demote all historical evidence, and state in the abstract that the mechanism passively copies state by design.

## Reviewer 3 — “The inferential and reproducibility claims are overstated”

The manuscript uses Student t intervals over 21 fold scores, permutation tests, and fixed-fold bootstraps, but the folds are generated from a common model architecture and potentially correlated time-series features. “Original world” is a better unit than pixels, but it does not guarantee exchangeability. The validity filter further changes the target population. No equivalence margin was declared, so failed lower bounds cannot support a negative ownership statement.

The access-scope test has multiplicity and model-selection concerns. Four pairwise exclusions must pass, but the family-wise behavior of this conjunction is not characterized. A strict conjunction can be useful as a gate, yet it creates high false-negative probability. The paper should distinguish “did not meet a conservative sufficiency criterion” from “failed ownership.” At several points the latter wording sounds ontological.

The exact raw-only match is less impressive than presented. An implementation can independently reproduce a specification and still share conceptual mistakes, raw-data defects, or ambiguous field definitions. Reporting 9,357 matching leaves gives a false sense of sample size; there is one dataset and one reanalysis. The authors should not imply replication.

The DOI verification and hash registries are useful but do not establish scientific validity. The final seal binds whatever was present, including possible errors. The authorization-template bug illustrates this point. Moreover, author identity, affiliations, conflicts, and funding are blank, so the package is not actually ready for external review.

The figures appear designed to persuade by categorical color: green pass, red fail, and an Outcome B box. The uncertainty panels should show individual world scores and the decision figure should make clear that the labels arise from author-defined gates. The supplement should list all invalidity reasons by world. Without those additions, I recommend major revision.
